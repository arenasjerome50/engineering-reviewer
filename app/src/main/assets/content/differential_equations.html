Differential Equations

Differential Equation (DE) - is an equation containing at least one derivative.

Examples:

  1. $(2xy + {x^2})dx + 2ydy = 0$

  2. ${\left( {{{{d^3}y} \over {d{x^3}}}} \right)^2} - x{\left( {{{{d^2}y} \over {d{x^2}}}} \right)^3} + {x^3} = 0$

  3. $(x - 1)y'' + xy' + y = 0$

  4. ${{\partial x} \over {\partial y}} + 2y{{\partial x} \over {\partial z}} = 3$

Types of Differential Equations (DE):

  1. Ordinary DE - an equation containing only one independent variable, thus having only ordinary derivatives in the equation.
    (Examples a, b & c)

  2. Partial DE - an equation containing two or more independent variables, thus having partial derivatives in the equation.
    (Example d)

Order of a DE:

The order of a DE is the order of the highest ordered derivative which appears in the equation.

Degree of a DE:

The degree of a DE is the degree (exponent) of the highest ordered derivative which appears in the equation.

Example:
a - First order, First degree
b - Third order, Second degree
c - Second order, First degree
d - First order, First degree

Types of solutions of a DE:

  1. General solution - the solution has at least one arbitrary constant.

    Example: $x^{3}+3y=C$, where: C = arbitrary constant

  2. Particular solution - the solution has no arbitrary constant.

    Example: $2xy+y^{2}-2=0$

Solutions to first order DE:

  1. Variable separable type

    Standard form: $P(x)dx+Q(y)dy=0$

    General solution: $\int {P(x)dx} + \int{Q(y)dy} = C$

  2. Homogeneous type

    Standard form: $M(x,y)dx+N(x,y)dy=0$

    General solution: Substitute y = vx or x = vy and the resulting DE becomes a variable separable type

    Test for homogeneity of degree n:
    Let: $f(x,y) = M(x,y)dx+N(x,y)dy$
    $f(rx,ry)=r^{n}f(x,y)$

  3. Exact type

    Standard form: $M(x,y)dx+N(x,y)dy=0$

    General solution: $F(x,y) = C$

    where: $F(x,y)= \int{M(x,y)\partial x+k_{1}}$
    $F(x,y)= \int{N(x,y)\partial y+k_{2}}$

    Test for exactness: $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$

  4. Linear type

    Standard form: $\frac{dy}{dx}+yP(x)=Q(x)$

    General solution: $y(i.f)=\int{Q(x)(i.f.)dx}+C$

    where: i.f. = integrating factor = $e^{\int{P(x)dx}}$

  5. Bernoulli's equation type

    Standard form: $$\frac{dy}{dx}+yP(x)=y^{n}Q(x)$$

    General solution: $$y^{1-n}=\frac{1}{e^{\int{(1-n)P(x)dx}}}\int{(1-n)Q(x)e^{\int{(1-n)P(x)dx}}dx}$$

Application of first order DE

  1. Population growth problems

    $$\frac{dP}{dt}=kP$$

    where:
    $\frac{dP}{dt}$ = rate of change of the population
    P = number of inhabitants at any time t
    k = constant of proportionality

  2. Decay problems

    $$\frac{dQ}{dt}=kQ$$

    where:
    $\frac{dQ}{dt}$ = rate of change of the substance
    Q = amount of the substance present at any time t
    k = constant of proportionality

  3. Continuous compound interest problems

    $$\frac{dP}{dt}=rP$$

    where:
    $\frac{dP}{dt}$ = rate of change of the account
    P = money present in the account at any time t
    r = nominal rate of interest per year

  4. Flow problems

    $$\frac{dQ}{dt}=\text{Rate of gain}-\text{Rate of loss}$$

    where:
    Q = concentration of the mixture at any time t
    $\frac{dQ}{dt}$ = rate of change of the concentration of the mixture

  5. Cooling and heating problems

    Cooling process: $$\frac{dT}{dt}=k(T-t_{s})$$

    Heating process: $$\frac{dT}{dt}=k(t_{s}-T)$$

    where:
    $\frac{dT}{dt}$ = rate of change of the body temp.
    T = temperature of the body at any time t
    $t_{s}$ = surrounding temperature

  6. Newton's second law of motion

    $$F=m\frac{dV}{dt}$$

    where:
    F = force
    m = mass of the body
    $\frac{dV}{dt}$ = rate of change of velocity

  7. Geometrical problems (Orthogonal trajectory)

    A curve which intersects all curves of a given family at the same angles is referred to as a trajectory; if the intersection is at right angle, the curve is called orthogonal trajectory.

    $$\left ( \frac{dy}{dx} \right )_{t}=-\left ( \frac{dx}{dy} \right )_{g}$$

    where:
    $$\eqalign{
    & \left ( \frac{dy}{dx} \right )_{t} = \text{slope of the orthogonal trajectory} \cr
    & \left ( \frac{dy}{dx} \right )_{g} = \text{slope of the given family of curves}\cr
    & \left ( \frac{dx}{dy} \right )_{g} = \frac{1}{\left ( \frac{dy}{dx} \right )_{g}} \cr}$$
